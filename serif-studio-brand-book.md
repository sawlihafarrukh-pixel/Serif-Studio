Serif Studio designs brand identities for start-ups and small businesses, mostly in Malaysia and Pakistan, occasionally beyond. We are small on purpose. ## Chapter 1 · Mission

### Demonstration

> *Set in display serif, 96 pt, ranged left, sitting at the bottom of the spread.*

# We work with  
# people who are  
# building something  
# small, and serious.

### Philosophy

Most of our clients are one or two people deep into a business they've been thinking about for years. They don't need a brand that looks like a unicorn's brand. They need a brand that survives the first ten customers, the first wholesale email, the first time someone asks for the logo at 11 pm. We work for that brand. Quietly, precisely, and as if our name is on it — because, on the case study page, it is.

### Mission

To give small brands the kind of identity work that bigger ones usually keep to themselves: strategic, considered, and built to last past launch.

### What this means in practice

- We turn away projects we don't believe in. A bad fit is worse than no fit.
- We send fewer concepts and defend them harder. Two, not five.
- We finish what we start, including the file naming, the WhatsApp handover, and the social templates the founder will use on a Sunday night.
- We write things down so the brand keeps working when we're not in the room.

### Edge cases

When a brief conflicts with the mission — a vanity rebrand, an identity for a product we wouldn't recommend to a friend — Sawliha decides. The answer is allowed to be no.

## Chapter 2 · Logo

### Demonstration

> *The four-point spark, set 60 mm tall, centred on a page of nothing else. No caption. The mark explains itself.*

### Philosophy

The mark is a four-point spark — a crossed-light shape we draw with equal-length arms and a soft, slightly convex curve where they meet. It is the moment in a client conversation when the brand becomes obvious. We made it a star, because that is how it feels; we made the points soft, because that is how we work.

### Construction

```
Arm length:        equal on all four axes
Centre curve:      8% inward bow on each quadrant
Stroke:            solid fill, no outline
Optical centre:    1.5% above geometric centre
Construction grid: 12 × 12 units, mark fills 10 × 10
```

### Clear space

Clear space is one arm-length (`x`) on every side. Nothing — no type, no rule, no edge — enters this zone. When in doubt, give it more.

```
┌────────────────────────┐
│   ←x→                  │
│         ✦              │  ←x→
│                        │
│   ←x→                  │
└────────────────────────┘
```

### Minimum sizes

| Surface          | Minimum height |
|------------------|----------------|
| Print            | 8 mm           |
| Digital (UI)     | 16 px          |
| Favicon          | 16 × 16 px     |
| Email signature  | 32 px          |
| Embroidery       | 12 mm          |

### Colour variants

Three lockups, in order of preference:

1. **Primary** — Rust on Mist (the lockup on Behance and Linktree).
2. **Mono-light** — Rust or Espresso on cream / white.
3. **Mono-dark** — Mist or Cream on Espresso / black.

We do **not** reverse the mark to Mist on Rust at small sizes — the stroke optically thins and the spark looks bent.

### Background test

The mark sits cleanly on flat colour from the palette, on the dusty cream paper texture we use for case studies, and on photography where the focal area behind it is uncluttered.

- ✓ On the studio Mist circle, with at least 30% Mist visible around the mark.
- ✓ On any palette flat colour, at full contrast.
- ✓ On the bottom-left of a photograph where the upper two-thirds is the subject.
- ✗ On a photograph where the mark sits on a face, a product label, or text.
- ✗ Inside a coloured square that fights the palette (e.g., Rust mark on Blush).
- ✗ At an angle, tilted, or rotated for "playfulness." The spark stands up straight.

### Do / Don't

| Do | Don't |
|----|-------|
| Use the Mist circle lockup as the default profile mark. | Don't outline the mark. |
| Pair the mark with the wordmark only when both can breathe (clear-space respected). | Don't add a drop shadow, glow, or bevel. |
| Recolour to a single approved palette colour. | Don't recolour to a non-palette colour, even client palette. |
| Scale proportionally. | Don't stretch, skew, or letter-space the wordmark. |
| Crop the mark off-page for editorial covers. | Don't crop the mark in a way that loses an arm. |

### Forbidden uses (with red-lines)

- Tilted spark · ✗
- Outlined spark · ✗
- Spark inside a square the same hue as the spark · ✗
- Spark with a tagline lockup we haven't approved · ✗

### Edge cases

When a client co-brand is required (a collaboration, a sponsorship, a co-authored case study), the marks sit side by side with a vertical hairline rule between them, separated by 2x of the larger mark's clear space. Sawliha approves all co-brand lockups before publication.

## Chapter 3 · Colour

### Demonstration

> *A full-bleed spread divided into five horizontal bands — Rust, Mist, Cream, Espresso, Blush — each band labelled at the right edge in 8 pt caps. No body copy. The palette is the page.*

### Philosophy

Our palette is a room, not a paintbox. Rust is the seat by the window — where most of the light falls, where the eye goes first. Mist is the wall behind it: it gives Rust somewhere to rest. Cream is the floor. Espresso is the door frame, used sparingly, holding everything in place. Blush is a flower on the table — present in roughly one of every five rooms, never the whole room.

We do not use all five colours together on one page often. Two or three, well-spaced, will almost always do.

### Core palette

#### Rust — the hero

A warm terracotta. Confident, hand-made, slightly Mediterranean. The colour of the spark.

```
HEX     #B85C2C
RGB     184, 92, 44
CMYK    20, 70, 90, 8
PMS     PMS 1675 C
```

Use for: the spark, primary CTAs, key headlines on cream, the closing line on a case study, the colour of the studio's voice.

#### Mist — the steady

A dusty, cool-leaning blue-grey. The colour of the Linktree circle and the Behance avatar. Calm, considered, never cold.

```
HEX     #9DB4BC
RGB     157, 180, 188
CMYK    40, 18, 22, 8
PMS     PMS 5435 C
```

Use for: the studio's profile background, secondary blocks, large quiet fields, the "before" half of a before/after.

#### Cream — the floor

A warm, paper-leaning off-white. Our default page colour. Almost everything sits on this.

```
HEX     #F2EBE0
RGB     242, 235, 224
CMYK    4, 6, 12, 0
PMS     PMS Warm Gray 1 C
```

Use for: the default canvas, page backgrounds, large negative space, body type backgrounds.

#### Espresso — the frame

A deep, slightly warm brown-black. Used sparingly. Holds the palette together when contrast is needed.

```
HEX     #2E1F17
RGB     46, 31, 23
CMYK    65, 75, 78, 88
PMS     PMS Black 7 C
```

Use for: body type, captions, the frame line on case studies, the back cover.

### Secondary palette

These three exist to support the core, never to lead. They appear in roughly one in five compositions.

#### Blush

```
HEX     #E5C5B0
RGB     229, 197, 176
CMYK    8, 24, 28, 0
PMS     PMS 4685 C
```

The colour of the Discovery form. Use for soft fields, warmth around editorial photography, the inside of an envelope.

#### Olive

```
HEX     #7D7A4A
RGB     125, 122, 74
CMYK    45, 38, 80, 30
PMS     PMS 5757 C
```

Borrowed from the Brunch Out project but kept in the system. Use for food, lifestyle, brands that want a grounded, slightly herbal feel.

#### Honey

```
HEX     #C9A063
RGB     201, 160, 99
CMYK    20, 38, 70, 8
PMS     PMS 7563 C
```

The Maeven gold, kept for jewellery, beauty, and packaging accents. Never use as a flat full-page field.

### Colour balance — what a typical surface should hit

A typical Serif Studio page or post sits roughly at:

```
Cream         55%   ████████████████████████████
Rust or Mist  25%   █████████████
Espresso       7%   ████
Blush/Olive/   8%   ████
Honey
Other          5%   ███
```

If a layout drifts past 40% Rust or past 40% Mist, pause. Add Cream.

### Dark mode (case study microsites)

When dark mode is needed, Espresso (`#2E1F17`) becomes the canvas and Cream (`#F2EBE0`) becomes the type. Rust shifts one stop lighter to **Ember `#D87A4E`** (4.9:1 AA on Espresso) to stay legible at small sizes.

### Do / Don't

| Do | Don't |
|----|-------|
| Use Cream as the default page colour. | Don't default to pure white — it makes the palette go cold. |
| Use Rust to draw the eye, once per page. | Don't repeat Rust three times on a single screen; it loses its job. |
| Pair Espresso type with Cream backgrounds. | Don't set Espresso type on Mist below 18 pt. |
| Use Blush, Olive, Honey as accents. | Don't make any secondary colour the dominant field. |
| Break the rules when a client's identity demands it — and say so. | Don't quietly import client colours into our brand templates. |

### Edge cases

When a client project legitimately requires a colour outside our palette (e.g., a luxury jewellery brand that needs deep emerald), use it inside the client's work. Do not use it on our case study chrome — the frame stays Serif Studio.

## Chapter 4 · Typography

### Demonstration

> *Set Canela Display at 160 pt, ranged left, with descenders kissing the bottom margin.*

# A serif walks into  
# a room and the  
# room calms down.

### Philosophy

We are called Serif Studio. The name is a promise: we believe a well-cut serif does a kind of work nothing else does. It signals time, intention, and a maker who chose the letterform on purpose. We pair it with a quiet sans that gets out of the way.

### The system

**Display — Canela**  
*Commercial Type · licence: web + print, 2 weights.*  
Use for: headlines, mission statements, case-study openers, the cover.

Free fallback: **Playfair Display** (Google Fonts).

**Text — Söhne**  
*Klim Type · licence: web + print, 3 weights.*  
Use for: body copy, captions, UI, anything below 24 pt.

Free fallback: **Inter** (rsms.me).

If you can only license one face for a small project, license Canela and pair it with the system sans. Never the other way around.

### Weights in use

| Face            | Weight       | Where it lives                                    |
|-----------------|--------------|---------------------------------------------------|
| Canela Display  | Light        | Headlines 64 pt+, case-study openers              |
| Canela Display  | Medium       | Subheads 32–60 pt                                 |
| Söhne Buch      | Regular 400  | Body 10–18 pt                                     |
| Söhne Kräftig   | Medium 500   | Buttons, table headers, in-line emphasis          |
| Söhne Mono      | Regular      | Code, technical specs, file paths, this manual    |

We do not use Bold of Söhne. We do not use Canela below 24 pt. We do not use Canela italics for body copy.

### Type scale (print, A4)

```
Display XL    120 / 110     Canela Light       cover, chapter openers
Display L      72 /  72     Canela Light       section openers
Display M      48 /  52     Canela Medium      subheads
Heading        24 /  30     Canela Medium      page headings
Subheading     18 /  26     Söhne Medium       in-page sections
Body L         14 /  22     Söhne Regular      reading copy
Body           11 /  18     Söhne Regular      reading copy, default
Caption         9 /  14     Söhne Regular      captions, footnotes
Mono            9 /  14     Söhne Mono         specs, code
```

### Alphabet specimens (each weight)

> *Layout note: full alphabet uppercase + lowercase + numerals + currency + diacritics needed for Bahasa Malaysia, Urdu transliteration, English. One specimen per weight per spread.*

```
Canela Light · 96 pt
ABCDEFGHIJKLMNOPQRSTUVWXYZ
abcdefghijklmnopqrstuvwxyz
0123456789 & $ € £ ¥ ₨ RM
áéíóú ñ ç ž
```

### Alignment

- **Left-ranged** for almost everything. The English- and Malay-language baseline.
- **Centred** only on covers, ceremonial spreads, and the closing line of a case study.
- **Justified** never. The rag is part of the brand.
- **Right-ranged** only in side captions or right-edge labels.

### Forbidden treatments

- ✗ Canela in ALL CAPS. The letterforms were cut for mixed case.
- ✗ Söhne as a headline above 32 pt. It loses its job.
- ✗ Letter-spacing Canela below 0. The font is metricked correctly.
- ✗ Underlining for emphasis. Use italic or a Rust colour shift.
- ✗ Drop caps unless inside a long-form essay (3 paragraphs or more).

### Display vs. text — where each face belongs

Canela does the talking when there is one thing to say. Söhne does the talking when there are many things to say. If you find yourself setting Canela for a paragraph, the paragraph is too long, or the type is wrong.

### Do / Don't

| Do | Don't |
|----|-------|
| Set Canela Light on cream for chapter openers. | Don't set Canela on a busy photograph without a flat overlay. |
| Use Söhne Mono for specs and file paths. | Don't use Söhne Mono for body copy. |
| Range left, with a soft rag. | Don't justify and hyphenate. |
| Let headlines sit on the baseline grid. | Don't bump leading "for breathing room" — fix the type size instead. |

### Edge cases

For brands we design who already have a typography system, our manual sets the brand chrome (case study covers, the watermark, captions) in Serif Studio type — the client work uses theirs. The two faces never share a page.

## Chapter 5 · Photography

### Demonstration

> *A single photograph: a hand placing a folded postcard on a cream linen surface, light coming from one side, no model, no face. Captioned in 9 pt mono at the bottom: "Light from the left. One subject. One quiet decision."*

### Philosophy

We photograph the way we design — one subject, soft daylight, a calm surface. We rarely show faces. We almost never show product on white seamless. The photographs we take and commission look like they could be the eleventh photograph in a long-running editorial column. They are not advertising.

### Casting & subject

- **Hands, not faces.** A hand holding a card. A hand pouring a coffee.
- **Objects, lit naturally.** The product on the surface it actually lives on.
- **People, occasionally.** Three-quarter back, mid-action, never staring at camera.

### Lighting

- Daylight, soft, one direction.
- Shadows are part of the photograph — never cloned out.
- No ring lights. No studio strobes mimicking daylight.

### Composition

- One subject. The eye should know what to look at within half a second.
- Negative space is part of the photograph. Crop generously around the subject.
- Camera height: above the table for objects; eye-level for people.
- Surfaces: linen, untreated wood, cream paper, a single colour from the secondary palette.

### Colour grading

Slight warm cast. Reds and oranges read true; blues stay dusty. Highlights never blown to pure white — Cream `#F2EBE0` is the brightest point in any photograph.

### Do / Don't

| Do | Don't |
|----|-------|
| Photograph the product on cream linen, daylit. | Don't use stock photography. Anywhere. Ever. |
| Crop generously — let the subject sit in space. | Don't fill the frame edge-to-edge with the product. |
| Show hands and objects in mid-action. | Don't shoot models staring into the camera. |
| Keep shadows. | Don't retouch shadows out — they are the light. |

### Edge cases

When a client supplies their own photography and it doesn't match this style, we don't reshoot at our cost; we treat their photography honestly inside their identity. The case study cover shot is Serif Studio's — we shoot one image per project that we control.

## Chapter 6 · Illustration & ornament

### Demonstration

> *The four-point spark, scattered across a page in three sizes — 8 mm, 16 mm, 32 mm — arranged off-grid. A single line of caption: "The spark is also an ornament."*

### Philosophy

We don't illustrate scenes. We use a small vocabulary of marks — the spark, a hairline rule, a soft hand-drawn leaf reserved for botanical and beauty brands — and we let them work hard. Illustration is ornament, not narrative. If a story needs to be told visually, we tell it with photography or typography.

### The ornament set

1. **The spark.** Same as the logo mark. Used at 8–32 mm as a divider, a list bullet, or a closing flourish.
2. **The hairline rule.** 0.25 pt, Espresso, full-width or short-stop. Used to separate ideas without shouting.
3. **The leaf.** Hand-drawn, single-stroke, only used on beauty, botanical, food, or wellness brands. Never on professional services or tech.

### Construction rules

- All ornament is monoline — a single weight throughout.
- Ornament is set in palette colours only.
- The spark is never combined with the leaf on the same page; one ornament per surface.

### Do / Don't

| Do | Don't |
|----|-------|
| Use the spark as a section closer at 16 mm, centred. | Don't draw new ornaments without Sawliha's sign-off. |
| Use the hairline rule to separate ideas. | Don't use the hairline rule as a frame around the whole page. |
| Use the leaf for beauty / food / wellness. | Don't use the leaf on tech, B2B, or professional services. |

## Chapter 7 · Iconography

### Demonstration

> *A grid of 12 icons at 32 px, all set in the same monoline weight.*

### Philosophy

Our icons are quiet. We use them where words would crowd: form steps, navigation, status indicators. We do not use icons to decorate.

### System

```
Grid           24 × 24 px
Stroke         1.5 px
Corners        2 px radius
Terminals      flat (no caps)
Colour         Espresso, or palette colour matching the surrounding type
Style          outline only — no filled icons in the system
```

### Library (minimum)

- arrow-right, arrow-down, arrow-up-right (external link)
- check, x, alert
- mail, phone, message (WhatsApp & DM contexts)
- calendar, clock
- file, folder, download

### Do / Don't

| Do | Don't |
|----|-------|
| Use outline icons at 1.5 px stroke. | Don't mix outline and filled icons in one layout. |
| Match icon colour to its label. | Don't put a Rust icon next to an Espresso label. |
| Use one icon per row in lists. | Don't bullet a list with both an icon and a spark — pick one. |

## Chapter 8 · Grid & layout

### Demonstration

> *A spread showing a 12-column grid in 5% Mist, overlaid with a real case study page — the page sits on the grid, the grid is visible, the relationship is the point.*

### Philosophy

The grid keeps things calm. We use a 12-column grid because it divides into the most useful pieces (halves, thirds, quarters, sixths). Most pages use fewer than six columns of actual content — the rest is air.

### Print grid (A4 portrait, 210 × 297 mm)

```
Page size              210 × 297 mm
Outer margin           20 mm
Inner (binding) margin 24 mm
Top margin             24 mm
Bottom margin          22 mm
Columns                12
Column gutter          4 mm
Baseline grid          4 mm
```

### Web grid

```
Max content width      1200 px
Columns                12
Gutter                 24 px
Outer margin (desktop) 64 px
Outer margin (mobile)  20 px
Baseline grid          8 px
```

### Layout patterns

- **Cover / chapter opener** — single column, headline at the bottom, mark at the top-left corner.
- **Reading page** — 7 columns of body, 5 columns of air, captions in the air.
- **Case study spread** — image bleeds the right page, type sits on the left in 5 columns.

### Do / Don't

| Do | Don't |
|----|-------|
| Let images bleed off the page. | Don't bleed type off the page. |
| Use the baseline grid for body type. | Don't fight the grid to fit a "good" headline length — cut the headline. |
| Leave at least 30% of every page as air. | Don't fill margins with decoration. |

## Chapter 10 · Motion

> *PDF documentation: motion is shown as a 5-frame storyboard with arrows and timing notes.*

### Demonstration

> *A 5-frame strip showing the spark fading in, then a headline rising 8 px into place, then the body fading in last. Annotated with timing.*

### Philosophy

We don't animate to entertain. We animate to signal — that something is finished, that something is loading, that something has been received. Motion is a punctuation mark.

### Principles

1. **The spark moves first.** It is the brand's heartbeat.
2. **Type rises, never falls.** Headlines enter from below by 8 px.
3. **One thing at a time.** No simultaneous entrances of multiple unrelated elements.
4. **Easing is gentle.** We use `cubic-bezier(0.22, 0.61, 0.36, 1)` — fast in, slow out.

### Default durations

```
micro       120 ms     hover, focus
fast        200 ms     button press, small state change
default     320 ms     page section reveal
slow        560 ms     hero entrance, cover sequence
```

### Storyboard — case study page hero

```
Frame 1  (0 ms)        Cream surface. Nothing yet.
Frame 2  (0–200 ms)    Spark fades in from 0 to 100% opacity.
Frame 3  (200–520 ms)  Headline rises 8 px into place, fades in.
Frame 4  (520–840 ms)  Body fades in, no movement.
Frame 5  (840 ms)      Settled. Cursor enabled.
```

This sequence signals *arrival* — the work is here, it has been waiting for you, it is calm.

### Forbidden motion

- ✗ Bouncing eases (the spark does not boing).
- ✗ Spinning the spark for loading. Use a hairline progress bar in Rust.
- ✗ Parallax. We are not a 2014 portfolio site.

## Chapter 11 · Tone of voice

### Demonstration

> *Set Canela Light, 96 pt, ranged left:*

# Write the way  
# you'd talk to a  
# founder over coffee.

### Philosophy

We are warm, but we are not cute. We are professional, but we are not corporate. We are confident, because confidence is what the founder is hiring. Our voice should sound like one person who knows what they're talking about, takes the work seriously, and takes themselves a little less seriously than that.

### The four principles

1. **Direct.** Say the thing. Cut the soft opener.
2. **Specific.** Numbers and nouns over adjectives.
3. **Warm.** Contractions, second person, the occasional first.
4. **Quiet.** Headlines are short. We do not shout.

### Seven tactics — how to write Serif Studio

1. **Cut the first sentence.** If you wrote "We're excited to share that…", delete it. Start with what is true.
2. **Address the reader directly.** "Your business" beats "the business." "We" beats "the studio."
3. **Use one number per paragraph.** A real number anchors a paragraph.
4. **Use contractions.** "We're" not "we are." Manuals that sound like contracts are not ours.
5. **End with the next step, not a flourish.** "Here's how we start" beats "We can't wait to hear from you."
6. **Read it aloud.** If it doesn't sound like you, rewrite.
7. **Resist exclamation marks.** One per project, maximum. Usually zero.

### The four contexts

#### Context 1 · Standing out (marketing, social, the IG grid)

The voice when we are introducing ourselves. Confident, slightly playful, a little surprising.

- Lead with a question or a contrarian observation.
- Make the headline carry. The caption is a footnote, not the message.
- Use specifics from real client work over generic claims.

**Before / after**

> ❌ "Looking for a beautiful new brand identity? We can help!"  
> ✓ "Can you get a full brand identity for the price of a coffee? Probably not. Here's what you actually get."

> ❌ "We design memorable brands."  
> ✓ "Your business is great. Your branding could be too."

> ❌ "Following best practices for brand strategy."  
> ✓ "From idea to identity. Here's how we do it."

#### Context 2 · Converting customers (DMs, the Discovery form, proposals)

The voice when someone has raised their hand and wants to know what happens next. Warm, structured, generous with information.

- Open by naming what they sent us.
- Tell them the next step in one sentence.
- Quote the timeline and the price without hedging.

**Before / after**

> ❌ "Thanks for reaching out! We'd love to learn more about your project."  
> ✓ "Thanks for the note. Tell me about the business and I'll send back a one-pager with timing and price by Friday."

> ❌ "Our packages start at competitive rates depending on scope."  
> ✓ "Brand identities start at RM 3,000. The Discovery form tells me what you need; the proposal tells you what it costs."

#### Context 3 · Adding delight (the small surprises — the gift, the close, the off-week post)

The voice when nothing is required and we are doing it anyway. Light, specific, never a meme.

- Specific over universal — a real client moment, not a generic platitude.
- Short. Delight is not a paragraph.
- No emoji. The spark is our emoji.

**Before / after**

> ❌ "Happy Friday from the Serif Studio team! 🎉🎉🎉"  
> ✓ "Maeven Studio's first sample arrived yesterday. The packaging weighs more than I expected. That's a compliment."

> ❌ "Wishing all our clients a great week!"  
> ✓ "Brunch Out hit 1,000 followers. The mustard yellow was right."

#### Context 4 · Providing reassurance (handover, edits, the part of the project where the founder gets nervous)

The voice when the project is in motion and the founder is worried about something. Calm, specific, evidence-based.

- Name the worry before answering it.
- Use a number or a date when you can.
- Close with the next thing you will do, not the next thing they should do.

**Before / after**

> ❌ "Don't worry, everything is on track!"  
> ✓ "The packaging review is later than I'd like. I have the revised dielines back by Monday and the final file to you by Wednesday."

> ❌ "We'll get back to you ASAP."  
> ✓ "I'm reading the feedback tonight and replying tomorrow morning with the edits sorted into yes / no / let's-talk."

### "Go global, write local" — language

We work in Malaysia and Pakistan, in English and increasingly in Bahasa Malaysia, with occasional Urdu-script transliteration in display work. Three rules:

1. **English copy expands ~30% in Bahasa Malaysia.** Headlines designed at 7 words break at 10. Design for the longest version, not the English version.
2. **Wordplay rarely translates.** "Brunch Out" is funny in English; it is meaningless in Bahasa. Transcreate, don't translate.
3. **Currency reads as `RM 3,000` or `PKR 210,000`** with a thin space, never `RM3000` or `Rs.210000`. Currency code first, space, amount, comma thousands separator.

**One headline, three languages**

> EN — Brands that feel considered.  
> MS — Jenama yang terasa difikirkan dengan teliti.  
> UR — برانڈز جو سوچ بچار کے ساتھ بنائے گئے ہوں۔ *(transliterated for display only; not used in body copy)*

### When you're stuck

DM Sawliha on WhatsApp. The brand voice is faster to debug in a 30-second voice note than in a deck.

## Chapter 12 · Grammar & style

### The list

- **Oxford comma** — yes, always.
- **Headline case** — sentence case. The only thing capitalised in a headline is the first word and proper nouns. Brand names are always capitalised as the brand prefers (LOA & FAYE, Maeven Studio, Brunch Out).
- **Dates** — `2 June 2026`. Day, month, year. No ordinals. No "the." International order, not US.
- **Times** — `09:30` or `9.30 am`. Never `9:30AM`.
- **Currency** — `RM 3,000` or `PKR 210,000`. Currency code, thin space, comma thousands separator.
- **Numbers** — one to nine in words; 10 and above as numerals. Always numerals in tables, specs, and prices.
- **Percentages** — `30%`, no space.
- **Contractions** — yes. "We're," "you'll," "it's." Manuals sound like people.
- **Ampersand** — only inside brand names (LOA & FAYE), business names (Spacing, padding & radius is fine in titles), and tight chips. Otherwise "and."
- **Em dash** — yes, with spaces on either side — like this — used sparingly.
- **Quotes** — curly ", ', not straight " '. Em dash for interruption, not double hyphen.
- **Lists** — sentence case bullets, no terminal punctuation unless the bullet is a full sentence.

### Words we use

Considered. Quiet. Specific. Honest. Crafted. Small. Patient.

### Words we avoid

Bespoke. Synergy. Curated. Disruptive. Reimagined. Solutions. Journey (unless literally a path on a map). World-class. Unique (everyone's unique). Effortless. Ecosystem. Best-in-class.

### Words we'd rather not say but sometimes do, with the alternative

| Tempting | Better |
|----------|--------|
| Stunning | Honest about what it is |
| Iconic | We don't get to call our own work iconic |
| Holistic | Full / end-to-end |
| Curated | Chosen / selected |
| Journey | Process / steps |
| Bespoke | Made for you / custom |

### Targets

# Section 4 — Patterns (worked examples)

Six representative scenarios. Each shows the situation, the design rationale, the final artefact, and the annotations that would otherwise live in a designer's head.

## Pattern 1 — A founder finds us and submits the Discovery form

**Situation** — A 28-year-old founder, three weeks into a skincare line, finds us via Instagram, reads three posts, taps Linktree, opens the Discovery form.

**Rationale** — Friction is the enemy here. The form must look like the brand (Blush surface, Rust button, Canela header) and read like a conversation. Every question must matter to the proposal.

**Artefact** — The existing form, retained as-is.

**Annotations**
- Section 1 is the warm-up — easy questions, no pricing yet.
- Section 2 reveals scope and budget — this drives the proposal.
- Section 3 reveals taste — drives direction.
- Section 4 closes — channel preference and any final notes.

## Pattern 2 — A case study page

**Situation** — A finished project (Maeven Studio) needs to go live on the studio site and on Behance.

**Rationale** — One project, one page, one closing line. The case study is a museum label, not a sales pitch.

**Spread structure**
1. Cover — client name in Canela, spark top-left, one-line role description.
2. The brief, in one paragraph.
3. The direction, in one paragraph and three reference images.
4. The system — logo, palette, type.
5. Application — six photographs, no captions.
6. Closing line — one Canela sentence.

**Annotations**
- We never show iteration. The final work is the case study.
- We do not embed video of the process. The work stands alone.
- The closing line is in Rust. Everything else is Espresso.

## Pattern 3 — A weekly IG series ("From Idea to Identity")

**Situation** — Six posts, one per week, walking founders through the studio's process.

**Rationale** — The series is the brand's clearest piece of educational marketing. Each post is a chapter; the series is a manual. Same headline grammar, same spark closer, same caption shape, so the grid reads as a single piece.

**Per post**
- Mode C template (Cream surface).
- Headline: "Week N · {step name}".
- Body: three lines.
- Spark closer.
- Caption: 80–140 words, one CTA.

## Pattern 4 — Pitch deck for a new prospective client

**Situation** — A founder asks for a deck before the Discovery call. We send a 10-slide deck that mirrors the brand book in miniature.

**Rationale** — The deck shows them what working with us feels like, not just what we charge. Two of the ten slides are pure typography, no images — those slides do more work than any of the rest.

**Slides**
1. Spark + their brand name in Canela.
2. "Brands that feel considered."
3. A 90-word paragraph: why we're here.
4. Our three past clients, logos only.
5. How we work — three steps, one sentence each.
6. What you get — a list, set in Söhne.
7. Price and timeline.
8. What we ask of you.
9. Two case-study photographs.
10. "Here's what happens next." A single sentence + a date.

## Pattern 5 — A small gift after handover

**Situation** — Project ends. The founder gets the files, the Brand Style Guide PDF, and (locally, when feasible) a physical thing in the mail.

**Rationale** — Delight, per Chapter 11. Specific, not generic.

**Artefact** — A single letterpressed card, Vellum White, 1-colour Rust spark on the front. On the back, a handwritten line from Sawliha. No promotional content.

## Pattern 6 — Out-of-home (a poster, a tote, a stamped shipping label)

**Situation** — Studio merchandise or print pieces that travel — totes for client gifts, stickers, a single-poster announcement of a new project.

**Rationale** — At distance, the brand reads as Rust + Cream + the spark. That's it. Type is optional; the spark is not.

**Specs**
- Tote — natural cotton, Rust 1-colour print, spark at 80 mm centred, no wordmark.
- Sticker — die-cut spark, Rust on Cream, 40 mm.
- Poster — A2, Cream, Canela headline at 200 pt, spark at the bottom-right corner.
