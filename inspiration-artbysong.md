# Inspiration · @artbysong — post design system

A breakdown of how artbysong structures their Instagram posts. Reference for future Serif Studio posts. Studied from 13 screenshots — two artisanal bakery case studies (Butterful Microbakery, Re:Bakesic) and one corporate-mode grid sweep.

**Date noted:** 2026-06-11

---

## 1 · The two modes

artbysong runs two visibly separate visual systems:

### Mode A — Artisan / illustrated (most relevant to Serif Studio)
Used for bakeries, small food brands, character-led identities. Warm pastel palette, hand-illustrated marks, scrapbook composition, narrative-led case study slides.

### Mode B — Corporate
Used for proptech, crystals, B2B groups. Polished blue gradients, clean sans type, mockup-heavy. Generic-looking by design — it's the corporate-deck aesthetic.

> The studio segregates these two modes completely. They don't blend. The artisan grid and the corporate grid look like two different studios.

For Serif Studio, **Mode A is the relevant inspiration** — same client tier (small businesses, food/bakery/skincare), similar warm earthy palette territory, similar case-study-led content strategy.

## 2 · The carousel architecture

A typical artbysong case study carousel runs 10–15 slides with this structure:

```
Slide 01   Hero reveal (logo on product photo, large)
Slide 02   Logo system grid (Main / Secondary / Wordmark / Monogram)
Slide 03   Logo design reasoning (inspiration → mark, annotated)
Slide 04   Design reasoning (narrative + character details)
Slide 05   Colour palette (big bento grid, hex codes, smiley details)
Slide 06   Typography (sometimes — varies)
Slide 07   Hero scene 1 (product photo with brand applied)
Slide 08   Hero scene 2 (storefront, window decals)
Slide 09   Mood board / application collage
Slide 10   Business card / packaging close-up
Slide 11   Animated logo reveal (video slide)
Slide 12   Closing / credit
```

Key idea: **the case study is an essay**, not a portfolio dump. Each slide is a chapter, with a labelled section header at the top.

---

## 3 · The signature visual moves

### a · The "tape label" section header
Every section slide opens with a horizontal pill-shaped label at the top centre, styled to look like masking tape with the section name written on it (e.g. "Business Card", "Colour Palette", "Logo Design", "Design Reasoning").

- The label has rounded ends, slight texture.
- Set in a warm script/serif (looks like Recoleta or a similar warm rounded serif).
- Always centred at the top of the slide.
- This is their **strongest grammar device** — it makes the carousel read as a chaptered book.

### b · Bento-grid colour palettes
Palette slides are laid out as a **Bento grid** (varying rectangle sizes, not equal bands).

- Each swatch has the name, hex code, and a tiny decorative element (smiley face / bear icon / character signature).
- Large hero colours get a big rectangle; supporting colours get smaller rectangles.
- The colour name is set in a stylised display serif at large size — the palette is editorial.

### c · Logo grids labelled by role
Multi-lockup logo displays use a clean grid with **small-caps category labels** in the top-left of each section:
```
MAIN LOGO
SECONDARY LOGO
WORDMARK
MONOGRAM
```

The labels are tiny (~10pt), tracked-out, in a clean sans. The marks below get the visual space.

### d · "Design Reasoning" inspiration → mark
A signature slide type: show the inspirations (small line-drawn icons of butter curls, butter knives, chef hats) with arrows pointing to the corresponding part of the final mark. Annotated like a textbook diagram.

This is **process transparency as content** — showing the reader why the choices happened. It's educational AND it justifies the price.

### e · Magazine-collage application slide
The application/mood board slide layers multiple elements at slight angles with rounded corners and subtle drop shadows:
- Product photos
- Sticker stickers (circle stickers with characters)
- Storefront mockups
- Business card mockups
- Wordmark in a corner

The effect is scrapbook-like. Charming. Twee.

### f · Animated logo reveal as a slide
One slide in the carousel is a video (visible from the mute icon). The animated logo reveal lives inside the still carousel.

### g · Honest client attribution
When they use the client's own product photography, they credit it: `Image credits to Re:Bakesic`. Small text, bottom-right.

### h · Curved / arched type
Wordmarks and supporting taglines arch over or curve around the central mark. Very vintage signage / bakery feel. Heavy use of `text-along-path`.

## 4 · The type system (inferred)

- **Display** — a warm rounded serif (Recoleta family / similar). Used for project titles, palette names, taglines.
- **Script** — a casual script used inside the "tape labels" at the top of each chapter slide. Slight handwritten feel.
- **Body** — clean humanist sans. Used for body explanations and case study narrative.
- **Small caps sans** — used for category labels (MAIN LOGO, COLOUR PALETTE).
- **Monospace** — hex codes.

Four distinct families per project. That's a lot — but it works because each one has a specific role and never crosses lanes.

---

## 5 · The palette tendencies

Across both bakery projects:
- Cream / butter / off-white as default canvas
- Warm earthy supports — terracotta, camel, sage green
- One soft pop colour — peach, dusty pink
- Almost no pure white or pure black

Colours used per project don't repeat across projects — each client gets a distinct palette but they all sit in the same warm "artisanal" colour family.

---

## 6 · Caption tone (inferred from on-slide narrative)

Captions and narrative are warm, descriptive, slightly long-form. Example from a slide:

> "The bear's body would be rounded and plump, similar to the shape of a scone. It also apply the scone texture to the whole bear look. Its form should convey a sense of coziness."

Notice:
- Present-tense descriptive
- Talks about the design *as if it's still happening* (rationale before evidence)
- Sentence-cased, not lowercase
- Doesn't shy away from process language
- Charming over polished — even the slight grammar imperfections feel warm

For Serif Studio, this is interesting because it's **more verbose than the Serif Studio voice allows**. The takeaway is the *transparency*, not the verbosity — Serif Studio could do the same "show the reasoning" slides but keep the body to 25 words instead of 60.

## 7 · What Serif Studio should borrow

These translate well without diluting the Serif Studio voice:

1. **Chapter-marker section headers.** Every section slide gets a label at the top. Serif Studio's version: replace the "tape label" with a `Söhne Mono · 14pt · tracking +80 · Espresso 70%` label + a 0.25pt hairline rule. Same grammar, Serif Studio chrome.

2. **Bento-grid palette slides.** Instead of horizontal stacked bands (the current Serif Studio default), try a bento layout with varying rectangle sizes weighted to the usage ratio. Allows more breathing room for the hero colour.

3. **Logo system grid** with role labels (`MAIN LOGO` / `SECONDARY` / `WORDMARK` / `MONOGRAM`). Useful for Maeven, Cookie Ninja, LOA & FAYE case studies — show range of lockups, not just one.

4. **"Design reasoning" inspiration-to-mark slides.** Show the inputs (small line drawings of the source references) with arrows to the parts of the final mark they informed. Educational + justifies the work.

5. **Animated logo reveal as one slide** in the carousel. Cookie Ninja's mark especially could benefit — the ninja throwing the cookie animates well.

6. **Image credits to clients** when using their photography. Small detail, but it signals craft and partnership.

7. **The case study as essay, not gallery.** Multi-slide carousels (10+) with a clear chapter structure beat shorter portfolio-dump carousels.

## 8 · What Serif Studio should NOT borrow

These would dilute the Serif Studio brand if copied:

- ❌ Hand-drawn doodles, smileys on swatches, character signatures. Too playful for Serif Studio's quiet, considered voice.
- ❌ "Tape strip" graphics, masking-tape texture. Twee, decorative — violates the "whitespace is the brand" rule.
- ❌ Magazine-collage scrapbook composition (multiple layered elements at slight angles). Serif Studio is one element per slide, on the grid.
- ❌ Arched / curved type. Canela doesn't curve. Wordmarks stay straight.
- ❌ Four typefaces per project. Serif Studio is Canela + Söhne, end of system.
- ❌ Pink pastel palette tones. Serif Studio's palette is warmer / earthier / less candy.
- ❌ Drop shadows on cards, rounded-corner everything. Serif Studio defaults to sharp corners; softness is reserved for warm contexts only.

---

## 9 · The translation principle

> **Borrow artbysong's architecture, not their surface.**

artbysong's strength is the way they *structure* a case study — chaptered, transparent, narrative-led, with consistent grammar across slides. The surface (hand-drawn, layered, scripty, pastel) is their voice, not Serif Studio's.

When taking inspiration from an artbysong post, ask:
- What's the *slide type* doing? (Section opener? Palette? Reasoning? Application?)
- What's the *visual grammar* that makes the carousel read as one piece?
- How would Serif Studio do this slide type — quietly, on a Cream canvas, with Canela + Söhne and the spark?

Don't ask:
- How can I get this scrapbook feeling into Serif Studio?
- Can I add a tape label or a smiley to the swatch?

The answer to those is no.

## 10 · Concrete next moves (when designing future Serif Studio posts)

If a future post asks for a multi-slide case study carousel, default to this artbysong-inspired structure:

```
01   Cover                       (Cream, big Canela, masthead)
02   The brief                   (logo + 25-word body)
03   Logo system                 (Main / Secondary / Wordmark / Monogram grid)
04   Logo reasoning              (inspiration → mark, annotated)
05   Colour palette              (Bento grid, hex codes, no smileys)
06   Typography                  (specimens, hierarchy)
07   Application 1               (full-bleed photo)
08   Application 2               (full-bleed photo)
09   Animated logo reveal        (video slide)
10   Closing                     (Cream, closing line, co-credit)
```

Slides 03 and 04 are **new patterns to add to Serif Studio's repertoire** — they're not currently in the brand book's pattern library, but they translate well.
